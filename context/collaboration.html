Collaboration in Computer Art

Working draft — a frame for the course's AI policy.

You never make computer art alone.

That sounds like a slogan, but it is closer to a plain description of the medium. The moment you write code you are working with something that has its own behaviors, defaults, and resistances — the language, the machine, the network, and the long history of forms you are (knowingly or not) answering. Coding is collaboration before any other person, or any AI, enters the room.

It helps to name the registers this collaboration comes in. Here are three, newest to oldest.

Three registers

1. Conversation with AI. The most recent. You work through an AI assistant — sounding board, foil, and chief coder. You describe, it proposes, you push back, it revises. The exchange itself becomes part of the making.

2. Frankencoding. Older. You have an idea and assemble it from parts — a snippet from StackOverflow, a library someone released, an example you found and bent to your purpose. You are collaborating with a community spread across time, through the code it left behind.

3. From scratch. Oldest. You hand-code every line yourself. This feels like working alone — but even here you collaborate with the medium: what the language makes easy or hard, how random() behaves, where the frame loop pushes back, what the canvas will and won't do.

The collaborators are always there

Notice what the three share. Whether you keep full authorial control or hand the keyboard to outside sources, you are negotiating with the affordances of computers, the quirks of a particular language, the accumulated work of a community, and the history of art itself. Autonomy is not the absence of collaboration; it is one setting on the dial.

The deepest version of this is language. No one invents a language from scratch to write a poem; you inherit a grammar, a vocabulary, and centuries of usage, and you make something new by collaborating with that inheritance. Writing code is the same: a programming language is a vast inherited system you did not build and could not rebuild alone. "From scratch" was never really from scratch.

This is why AI is not a clean break. It is a new register of something the medium has always required. If anything it is the most honest of the three about the fact of collaboration: writing from scratch hides your collaborators, while a conversation with AI makes the deferral impossible to ignore.

Reading won't cost you your voice

The collaborator makers most often resist is tradition. The worry runs: if I read other poets, or take apart other artists' systems, their voices will contaminate the one I'm trying to keep pure — so I protect mine by not looking.

It's worth knowing that the two most influential theorists of poetic originality, T. S. Eliot and Harold Bloom, disagreed about nearly everything except this. For Eliot, tradition is an order the new work joins and quietly rearranges; for Bloom, originality is a combat with strong predecessors. Neither imagined a third option in which you simply avoid them — influence, Bloom wrote, is the water all writers swim in. The question was never whether you engage what came before, only how.

And the fear gets the mechanism backwards. You can only avoid what you can recognize. Skip the reading and your "original" voice doesn't come from nowhere — it comes from the little you've absorbed without noticing: ad copy, song lyrics, the nearest clichés. Reading widely is what gives you both more choices and the awareness to catch yourself reaching for the obvious. Avoiding tradition is the surest route to being derivative — and to not knowing that you are.

So reading is not a threat to your voice; it is how a voice is built. Voice is made, not found. The more widely you read, the more your voice becomes yours — not an echo of one source, but a synthesis no one else could assemble. That is the same move this course asks of every system: not to express a self you already have, but to discover what becomes possible inside a tradition you've actually let in.

Collaborating is not the same as handing off

If collaboration is always present, the interesting question is no longer whether you used a collaborator — you always do — but how you collaborated. The registers are not equal in every respect. AI differs in scale (it can produce a whole system at once), in opacity (you may not see its sources or reasoning), and in how easily it invites abdication — handing over so completely that nothing is understood, shaped, or owned.

So the line that matters runs not between "solo" and "assisted," but between collaboration and abdication:

  • Collaboration — you engage, you push back, you understand what you keep, and you take responsibility for the result.
  • Abdication — you accept output you did not examine and could not explain.

That distinction holds across all three registers. You can frankencode thoughtlessly (paste until it runs) or thoughtfully (read, adapt, understand). You can treat AI as a partner or as a vending machine. The register is not the problem; the quality of the collaboration is.

Why this is the course's stance, not a special rule

This is simply discovery over expression applied to the question of authorship. The myth of the lone creator channeling a private vision through a transparent tool is the expression model. The view here — that making is a negotiation with systems that push back — is the discovery model the course runs on. You already practice it every time you treat random(), noise(), or a dataset as a collaborator with its own behavior. AI is one more such collaborator, continuous with the rest.

It also means the collaboration is part of the work, not scaffolding to hide. In a course where systems dramatize states, how you worked with your collaborators is itself something the work performs — which is why documenting the exchange (an annotated conversation.md) exposes the form rather than burdening it.

Where this leads

The course's AI policy is the practical half of this frame. Read this way, its rules stop being prohibitions and become collaboration literacy: prompt for constraints, not outcomes; curate what the system offers; keep nothing you can't explain; document the exchange. Those are not special precautions against AI. They are what collaborating well has always asked of you — now made explicit, because the newest collaborator makes the stakes harder to ignore.

Seed notes (original):

There are (at least) 3 levels of collaboration when it comes to making computer-based art. The most recently developed: collaboration through conversation with AI, where AI is the sounding board, foil, and chief coder. An older step: the human creator has an idea and cobbles together — frankencodes — and repurposes code from the community (StackOverflow, etc.). An older step: the human creator hand coding their idea from scratch. Whether the HC preserves their coding autonomy or defers the coding to outside sources, the HC is still collaborating with the affordances of computers, networks, quirks of particular languages (let alone the history of art). Like language [itself: an inherited system no one builds from scratch].
