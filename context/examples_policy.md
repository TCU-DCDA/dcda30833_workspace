# DCDA 30833 — Examples Policy (Instructor Reference)

Students will always ask for examples of the work they're expected to make.
That need is legitimate — dismissing it ("just discover!") breeds anxiety and
resentment. The goal is not to withhold examples but to be deliberate about
*which kind* you give, so the need is met without priming imitation.

This policy supports the course principles: **Form = Content · Systems
Dramatize States · Discovery over Expression.** See also
[meta_labs_projects.md](meta_labs_projects.md) (the Precedents / System Autopsy
beat) and [theory_foundations.md](theory_foundations.md).

---

## First: diagnose what they're actually asking

"Show me an example" is usually three different requests wearing one coat:

1. **Scope / format anxiety** — "How big? How finished? How long is the
   reflection? What does *done* look like?"
2. **Possibility anxiety** — "What can this even *do*? I can't picture it."
3. **Calibration anxiety** — "Am I doing the *right kind of thing*?"

Almost none of this is a request to copy. It is mostly logistical. So answer
scope and process generously, and stay disciplined only about the one thing
that breeds imitation: a single polished "right answer" to match.

---

## The core move: show systems and process, not surfaces and finals

1. **Show the rule, not just the result.** Prefer exemplars whose system is
   exposed — Sol LeWitt's instruction texts, Tyler Hobbs's *Flow Fields*
   write-up, Casey Reas's *Software Structures* descriptions. When the example
   is a *method*, copying the surface is beside the point.

2. **Show the range, never the single.** The antidote to imitation is
   multiplicity. Show many outputs of one algorithm (Fidenza's variations,
   LeWitt executed by different drafters). The lesson lands on its own: *the
   system is the work; the output varies.* This enacts Form = Content.

3. **Show process and failure, not polish.** Messy iterations, sketches, and
   dead ends calm scope anxiety and leave nothing finished to copy. Pull the
   spirit of the **Failure Harvest** lab forward.

---

## Concrete mechanisms

- **The disposable instructor demo.** Build a deliberately rough sketch live,
  narrating "this is *one* possibility, not the target." A visibly throwaway
  anchor reduces fear without becoming a template.
- **Off-axis examples.** For a color project, show a *sound* system with strong
  constraint logic. Students extract the transferable principle but cannot lift
  the surface.
- **Examples-as-autopsy with a deliverable.** Make viewing an example *require*
  recovering its rule (the autopsy questions). The example becomes analysis
  input, not a finish line.
- **Scope exemplars, explicitly labeled.** Show file structure, reflection
  length, canvas scale, rough time spent — answer the logistical questions
  head-on and separately from content. This absorbs most of the anxiety.
- **Prior student work — handle most carefully.** It is what they want most and
  the highest imitation risk. If you show it, pair each piece with *that
  student's reflection on what surprised them*, and show a spread rather than
  one "exemplary" piece. Foreground the discovery, not the artifact.

---

## Timing

This follows the unit rhythm **Lab → Autopsy → Project**:

- Address **scope anxiety up front** (format, length, file structure) — this is
  safe to give early and absorbs most of the worry.
- Hold the rich **content exemplars until after the lab**, when students have
  the vocabulary to recover a system rather than copy a look. (You cannot
  autopsy a system whose primitives you haven't met.)
- Optional: a brief **encounter** at the start of a mode — "here's a thing that
  exists, hold the question" — distinct from the full autopsy that follows.

---

## Name the norm out loud

Say it early and repeat it:

> *In this course, an example is a system you can take apart, not an image to
> reproduce. I will always show you how something was made; I will rarely show
> you one right answer to match.*

Setting that expectation converts the recurring ask from friction into the
course's actual method.
