AI in This Course — Policy & Practice

DCDA 30833 — Creative Coding for the Humanities

This course treats AI as one more system you work with — a collaborator that pushes back, like randomness, noise, and data — not an answer machine and not a shortcut. The point is the same as everywhere else in the course: what can this system reveal?

AI sits within a longer history of collaboration in computer art — you are always collaborating, with the language, the machine, the community, and the medium itself. This policy is the practical half of that frame; read it alongside Collaboration in Computer Art.

(Instructor note: this is the student-facing companion to examples_policy.md; both keep AI and exemplars in service of discovery over expression.)

The stance: AI is material, not a vending machine

  • You are the artist, the curator, and the critic. The system makes moves; you decide what to keep and why.
  • Discovery over expression. Ask "what can this system reveal?", not "make me a thing."
  • Form = content. When you use AI, the conversation and the curation are part of the work — not scaffolding you hide.

Where AI belongs — and where it doesn't

  • Do the labs by hand. Labs build your grammar (rules before randomness). Reaching for AI to complete a lab skips the very learning the lab exists to create. The one exception is the lab that is about AI: LAB 7 — Prompt as Material.
  • AI is welcome as material where a lab or project invites it — LAB 7, and projects that say so (PROJECT 4 onward). There it is not a crutch; it is a system to negotiate with and curate.
  • The test when unsure: Am I using AI to avoid understanding, or to discover something I will then understand and shape? The first isn't allowed; the second is the point.

Prompt for constraints, not outcomes

(Carried from LAB 7.) Describe rules and behaviors, not the finished look.

  • Good: "Use only circles and sin(); vary through rotation and scale."
  • Avoid: "Make a pulsing mandala." / "Make it look like the night sky."

The moment you specify what it should look like, you've stopped discovering.

Curate the machine

  • Generate more than you need — the creative act is selection.
  • Keep what surprises or interests you; be able to say what you rejected and why.

Stay critical — the Explanation Test

Before you keep anything AI gives you, you should be able to:

  1. Explain what it does and why it's there;
  2. Run it and see what it actually does (AI is confidently wrong sometimes);
  3. Recreate or modify it yourself.

If you can't do all three, don't submit it yet — understand it first. Never turn in code or text you cannot explain.

Show your collaboration

  • When a lab or project uses AI, keep a short conversation.md (an AI log): a few annotated moments — your prompt, what the AI gave you, and why it mattered (a surprise, a redirection, a failure, a discovery). LAB 7 already asks for this.
  • Credit AI in your README.md: which tool, used for what. State plainly that AI-generated material was reviewed, run, and shaped by you.

Honesty (this course is ungraded)

There are no points to game here, so there is nothing to "cheat" toward. The only thing unexamined AI use can cost you is your own learning. Be honest about what the system did and what you did — that honesty is part of the reflection the course runs on. Passing off output you didn't examine as your own discovery isn't a problem because of grades; it's a problem because it skips the work that makes you someone who can think with these systems.

A note on discovery

When you write code by hand, you make every decision. When you prompt an AI, you relinquish some control — and that's the point. The AI will make choices you wouldn't: some boring, some wrong, some genuinely surprising. Your job is to notice, respond, and curate. The conversation is the creative work.
